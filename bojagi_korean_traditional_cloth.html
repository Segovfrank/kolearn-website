Bojagi - Korean Traditional Wrapping Cloths

Bojagi is traditional korean wrapping cloth. Typically they have square patterns and can be made of multiple materials although commonly silk or ramie (plant native to eastern Asia) are used.

History

Known to be used since early Joseon Dinasty (1392-1910)

In traditional Korean folk it was believed that wrapping objects protected them with good luck. Early usages were tablecloths or covering for sutras. Bojagi were used in special occassions, like weddings.

Types

Royal Bojagi

Up to 1,650 bojagi could be made for a royal wedding. Royal Bojagi was not patchwork

Common Bojagi

Also known as jogakbo (조각보) or Min-bo. Jogakbo was made by commoners using multiple pieces of cloths called 조각 (jogak) like leftovers from Hanboks for example

Jogakbo

During Joseon dinasty, women that concentrated on domestic tasks such as weaving, produced wrapping cloths, beddings, tablecloths and more with leftover pieces. Every last bit was used and the jogakbo were made depending on the women's aesthetic views.

It was tradition to spend hours between grandmothers, mothers and daughters laughing, talking and weaving these beautiful cloths.


Nowadays, Jogakbo is an art of it's own. You can see many museums and galleries dedicated to this tradition.
